RED LINE EVALUATION

The first step we took for this re-design was evaluating the current website in an attempt to better understand the primary goals of the site and to also gain a sense of their branding. This also helps us understand how the information is laid out, and allows us to easily identified areas that can be improved.

KEY INSIGHT

Poor navigation/ Would benefit from use of an aligned grid/ Extensive use of large imagery/ Poor information architecture/ Primary function for users to visit the site/ is news/updates; secondary goal is a portal to other offices

INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

Based on our heuristic evaluation we knew that the information architecture needed to be evaluated and improved. This would build the framework for our new visual layout, but more importantly, it would also help improve the menu navigation. Using affinity mapping and card sorting, we began by listing each page and arranging them into a hierarchy that was clear and organized. We eliminated some categories by rearranging hierarchies that we felt could be simplified. These included combining Offices and Bureaus into a Directory tab, prioritizing Resources, and renaming Priorities to Commitments. The footer was link heavy and tall. We shortened it by combining links. Given that these items were low enough priority to be in tiny text at the bottom-most point of the page, we didn’t think it would hurt to hide a few. WIREFRAMES

At this point we started designing the interface with three things in mind: grid, navigation options, and layout. Our initial designs utilized a bottom navigation feature, which was beginning to become more popular with mobile web design. However, after early testing sessions we quickly changed to a standard header nav / hamburger style. The biggest reason to change the navigation structure was that we found the most common use cases for bottom navigation were interfaces that had few primary destinations.

SOME THOUGHTS ON HAMBURGER MENUS

Hamburger menus already exist in the user’s mental model; users know how they work. It also looks pretty sleek and gives more space to the content layout. I was once told that any time you have 5 or less primary destinations, you should aim to use bottom navigation. While I think that this is a solid foundation, I believe that guidelines of UX design are highly dependent on the individual use cases. Nothing in design should ever be one-size-fits-all.

STYLE GUIDE

AWe wanted to create a style guide that highlighted the brand vision and revitalized its appearance. We decided to go with neutral colours highlighted by forest greens, deep blues, and sunkissed yellows. For typeface, we wanted a look that could have passed for a newspaper headline yet also wanted to select a header that paired nicely with a modern and minimal font.
